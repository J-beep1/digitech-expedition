Immersion / Hook / Kick-off

1. Ender's Game — visceral experience

Someone has been watching you. They've been collecting data. And they've already made decisions about your future.

›

We open the unit with a read-aloud: Chapter 1 of Ender's Game. Ender's monitor is removed — the device that has been recording everything about him since birth. He doesn't know what the data said about him, who read it, or what they decided. We then ask: when does that start to sound familiar?

We follow it immediately with the Matrix waking-up scene. Both drop students into the same realisation from different angles: a system has been running around you, collecting data, making decisions, and you didn't know.

Ender's Game — Reading 1 Orson Scott Card, Ender's Game, Chapter 1 — read aloud before students encounter their own PAT data. Who has been collecting information about you, and what have they done with it?
The Matrix — Screening 1 ⚠ Parental guidance Wachowski Sisters, The Matrix (1999) — the waking-up scene. Neo discovers the comfortable world was the lie, and the real world is a body in a pod. The visceral version of Plato's Cave.

Case study 1

Golems and Computers

From ancient myth to modern machine — what happens when we build something that follows instructions but can't think?

›

Long before AI, the golem tradition asked what happens when humans create something that follows instructions but has no judgement of its own — and who's responsible when it goes wrong. We extend this through Plato's Cave: if the golem only sees the shadows it's shown, what does it think reality is? This grounds our later discussion of AI ethics in something ancient and human, not just technological.

Anchor texts Plato, The Republic, Book VII — the Allegory of the Cave; Jewish golem folklore (e.g. the Golem of Prague); selected passages on creator responsibility

Case study 2

Cybernetics and hyperreality

Feedback loops, systems that watch themselves, and fictions that become real

›

We look at how systems — thermostats, ecosystems, social feeds — regulate themselves through feedback. Then we ask a stranger question: can an idea behave like a system too? Hyperstition (fictions that make themselves real), Spencer-Brown's distinctions, Baudrillard's simulacra. The Matrix is our primary case study in hyperstition — a film about simulation and AI that then shaped how Silicon Valley talked about simulation and AI for twenty years. The fiction became the vocabulary.

Anchor texts Norbert Wiener, foundational cybernetics concepts; G. Spencer-Brown, Laws of Form (the distinction); Jean Baudrillard, Simulacra and Simulation (selected excerpts)
The Matrix — Screening 2 Wachowski Sisters, The Matrix (1999) — the Oracle's kitchen scene. A system that knows the future but chooses not to control you with it. Power operating from care rather than fear. "You're going to have to make a choice."

▸ Full detail — Digital Ethics: data, cybersecurity, sovereignty

Huizinga and Spencer-Brown do the same formal work from two angles, and that's what makes this week economical rather than five bolted-on topics. A distinction (Spencer-Brown) needs a boundary condition (Huizinga) to be legitimate, and it can fail in two ways: someone breaks the boundary (Baudrillard/hyperstition — already our case study), or the boundary breaks itself by re-entering its own space (Goodhart's Law).

The conceptual spine

  • Spencer-Brown — the cut (mechanism). "Draw a distinction and a world comes into being." An algorithm, a test, a data point IS a cut: correct/incorrect, in/out, flagged/clear. Taught stripped of jargon — a circle on the board, in/out, that's an algorithm.
  • Huizinga's magic circle — the boundary condition. A game is voluntary, bounded, serious within its bounds while making no claim outside them. PAT testing is a magic circle — "correct/incorrect" is real inside the test, and means nothing about the whole child outside it.
  • Baudrillard / hyperstition — the boundary violation (failure mode). Harm = the game-token escapes the circle and gets treated as unbounded truth. A PAT score used to describe "the real student" is the map mistaken for the territory. Phishing and AI scams are this violation manufactured — a fake magic circle built to extract what's only safe inside a real one.
  • Goodhart's Law — re-entry (why bias is structural, not moral). The score places the student, which reshapes teaching, which reshapes the next score — the distinction eating its own tail. Bias compounds with nobody lying.

Databases, cybersecurity, and bias aren't separate gaps — they're one story told three times: your data → who holds it → what picture it paints → who could exploit it.

The one-week sequence

  1. The cut — circle on the board, in/out = an algorithm. Students hunt the cut in familiar systems (traffic light, win condition, spam filter), then formalise PAT's cut.
  2. The circle — play something with an explicit boundary, then apply Huizinga to PAT. What happens when the score leaves the circle?
  3. The stat sheet — build an RPG-style character sheet from own PAT data (student / domain / score / date). Closing question: is your stat sheet you? Privacy: each student pulls only their own record.
  4. Manufactured circles — phishing spot-the-difference, MFA on a school-safe account, AI-scam case studies. All fake magic circles. Goodhart via "what happens when people learn the test."
  5. Whose circle, drawn how — CARE vs the extraction model. Students write and justify an attribution/protocol statement for their own artefact.

2. The mechanism stage

Hardware, circuits, binary, QR codes, software, real programming

›

Once we've asked the big questions, we ground them in how digital systems actually work — from a transistor to a working program. Students build a small text-based game (a "Zork-like") to learn control structures, then represent their logic as a flowchart.

  • Hardware and circuits — what's physically inside the machine
  • Binary and data representation — QR codes as a hands-on decoding exercise
  • Software and programming — writing real, working Python
  • Flowcharting — representing and tracing algorithms

Assessment 1 — Data

The stat sheet

A relational data model built from the student's own PAT data

›

Students analyse their own PAT assessment data — identifying patterns, strengths, and gaps — and model it relationally: student / domain / score / date, joined by ID. This is the databases content standard done for real, not a worksheet, and it's the same stat-sheet exercise from Digital Ethics (Case study 2) done properly, with a mark attached.

Using Python and visualisation tools, they turn the model into a personal learning dashboard that turns raw scores into insight and action. The closing question carries over from Digital Ethics: is your stat sheet you? The data is theirs; the question is what they do with it, and what it leaves out.

Privacy: each student works from only their own longitudinal record. If cohort data is used for the databases exercise, it's anonymised or fictionalised first.

Assessment 2 — Build

3. Choose your format, build the artefact

An open-format artefact built from the stat sheet — genre chosen by the student, and defended

›

The format is not decided in advance. Each student turns their own stat sheet into an interactive digital artefact and chooses what kind — a racer where scores become cars, a self-improvement app that logs progress, a dungeon crawler, a board game, something with no existing genre. Working in groups with assigned roles, sprint check-ins, a co-constructed rubric, and annotated code with flowcharts document the process. One philosophical annotation asks: "What distinction did I draw here, and what did it leave out?"

Deciding what the artefact is = drawing the circle. Huizinga and Spencer-Brown stop being decoration and become the assessed act — the DigiTech-vs-woodtech distinction, stated plainly to students: both make things to a brief, but data and rules are political in a way timber isn't.

Ender's Game — Reading 3 Orson Scott Card, Ender's Game, Chapter 14 — the game was always real. Read close to exhibition, when students are living inside their own projects. You were doing real things while thinking you were practicing.
The Matrix — Screening 3 Wachowski Sisters, The Matrix (1999) — Neo's enlightenment. The bullets slow down not because he has more power but because he has stopped being afraid. Fear was the operating system all along. The win condition is named: powerful tools, used from love rather than fear.
▸ Full detail — open format, mark differentiator, protocol statement

The mark differentiator (reward reasoning, not novelty)

  • Traditional transposition (e.g. scores → car stats) = competent, solid B.
  • Framework critique (e.g. a racer where the car gets slower as your "improvement" score rises, forcing the player to question what the metric rewards) = the format itself is the argument, reaches A.
  • A-band descriptor: "The format choice itself constitutes an argument about data and representation, and the student can defend it as such." A well-made game that says nothing is a B; a rougher game that knows what it argues and argues it through its form is an A. A traditional format done thoughtfully still scores well — this rewards the extra reach without punishing solid craft.

Wellbeing note — the self-improvement app option

The richest option (Goodhart turned reflexive — the student draws and lives inside their own metric) and the one to keep a light hand on. Framing that keeps it safe: not "optimise yourself" but "notice what the measure does to you when you live inside it." If a student's app uncritically gamifies self-judgment, that's the teachable moment — redirect toward interrogating the metric, not a mark-down.

Task stages (shared with Art — see Cross-curricular)

  1. Study (Art) — analyse how artefacts across formats make claims (sprites, car design, app UI, board pieces).
  2. Stat sheet (DigiTech) — this is Assessment 1, feeding directly into the build.
  3. Choose the format (shared) — decide what the artefact is, argue why. The reasoning itself is assessed.
  4. Design + render (Art) — develop the visual language the chosen format needs.
  5. Build (DigiTech) — code it: sequence, selection, iteration, functions.
  6. Protocols (shared) — attribution/protocol statement: data sources, assets, ICIP-aware, CARE-aware, plus one required line answering "why does it matter that my data stays confidential?" — the cybersecurity module's evidence point, folded in rather than separately assessed.
  7. Exhibit (shared) — curate and present with the statement. One artefact, both gradebooks.

Cross-curricular

Is it art?

Visual Arts and DigiTech, one artefact, both gradebooks

›

Using purposiveness as a minimum bar, we ask whether the artefact students designed and built can be art — and what that even means when its function is partly to play, and partly to express an idea. This isn't a side debate: the same cohort builds Assessment 2 in Art and DigiTech simultaneously, through a shared seven-stage task, with a single artist statement doubling as the DigiTech data-protocol statement.

Make it public

Exhibition

Presenting finished work to peers, other classes, and family

›

Learning that's never shown to anyone outside the classroom loses most of its motivating power. Students present their stat sheet and their chosen-format artefact publicly — explaining not just what they built, but why they chose that format, and what it cost them to learn it.

How it's run

Project management

Students manage their own work in small groups, the same way a real software team would.

Sprint check-ins

  • One leader per group, rotating each sprint
  • Weekly review of what shipped and what's blocked
  • Roles, decisions, and progress logged in the dashboard itself

Done criteria

  • Stat sheet live and displaying real, correctly-modelled PAT data insights
  • Chosen-format artefact functional, playable/usable, and visually intentional
  • Format choice argued and defended, not just made
  • Code fully annotated and matched to a flowchart

The tickable assessment artefact checklist now lives in Sprint HQ, so groups can check things off together as they go — see the Checklist tab there.
